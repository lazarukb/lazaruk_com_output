Moving to Linux, Selecting a Distribution

By Brad Lazaruk, Fri 14 June 2024, modified Fri 05 July 2024, in category Development

Debian, Linux, Linux Mint, pop!_os, Windows

Well, Microsoft finally went too far. And not just for me, although that’s certainly my focus here. Many people have noted that Microsoft’s proposed Recall product is the last straw, and we’ve moved away from Windows as a result.

For me, after over 30 years of using Windows, this was quite a decision. I have a lot of software and technical knowledge wrapped up in Microsoft server and desktop products. I am comfortable with Microsoft’s products. But the privacy violations that will be part of Recall is just too far.

After thinking about it a bit, I realised that most of my attachments to Windows are nostalgic and my reservations about Linux are likely dated. I don’t actually have a lot of software that is Windows specific, and some that I thought were actually have perfectly functioning workarounds for Linux, or Linux versions. Further, the problems that I had with Linux and *nix systems in general were from the 1990s or early 2000s and frankly they’ve been overcome now.

So with that in mind I downloaded some ISOs, updated my Oracle VirtualBox, and set out to run some evaluations on various distros. My original criteria for choosing a distro were that it be straightforward to install, be reasonable easy to add software from a repo GUI, be as close to mainstream as possible, and to smooth over my adoption of the new system, be able to look as much like Windows 11 as possible with the option to remove that in the future.
